</p><p>Microbial biosurfactants are surface-active substances synthesized by microorganisms such as bacteria, fungi, and yeasts. These compounds have both hydrophilic and hydrophobic moieties, allowing them to reduce surface and interfacial tension in liquids. Unlike synthetic surfactants derived from petroleum sources, microbial biosurfactants are biodegradable, eco-friendly, and non-toxic, making them an increasingly popular choice in diverse industries including agriculture, cosmetics, pharmaceuticals, food processing, petroleum, and environmental remediation.</p><p>
</p><p>One of the most well-known biosurfactants is rhamnolipid, predominantly produced by Pseudomonas aeruginosa. Other significant types include sophorolipids, surfactin, mannosylerythritol lipids (MELs), and trehalolipids. These compounds not only perform traditional surfactant roles such as emulsification and dispersion but also exhibit antimicrobial, antifungal, and antiviral properties.</p><p>
</p><p>Microbial biosurfactants stand out for their functionality under extreme conditions, including high temperatures, salinity, and varying pH levels, further enhancing their industrial appeal. As global industries seek sustainable alternatives, the biosurfactants market is gaining considerable traction.</p><p>
