<strong>MARKET DRIVERS</strong></p><p>
</p><p style="text-align:center"><strong>Rising Prevalence of Myopia to Accelerate Market Expansion</strong></p><p>
</p><p>Global surge in myopia cases, particularly among children and young adults, is creating substantial demand for low-concentration atropine treatments. Recent epidemiological studies indicate that myopia affects approximately 30% of the global population, with projections suggesting this could rise to 50% by 2050. Urbanization, increased screen time, and reduced outdoor activities are key contributors to this growing health concern. Low-concentration atropine (typically 0.01%) has emerged as a clinically proven therapy to slow myopia progression with minimal side effects compared to higher concentrations. This positions the treatment as a preferred option for long-term management of myopia in pediatric populations.</p><p>
</p><p style="text-align:center"><strong>Growing Adoption in Clinical Practice to Fuel Market Penetration</strong></p><p>
</p><p>Ophthalmology clinics and hospitals are increasingly incorporating low-concentration atropine into standard myopia management protocols, recognizing its efficacy and safety profile. </p><p>Furthermore, the treatment's favorable reimbursement scenario in developed markets improves patient access. Many private insurers and public healthcare systems now provide partial or full coverage for atropine therapy when prescribed for myopia control, removing a significant financial barrier to adoption.</p><p>
<strong>MARKET RESTRAINTS</strong></p><p>
</p><p style="text-align:center"><strong>Regulatory Hurdles and Approval Delays to Constrain Market Growth</strong></p><p>
</p><p>While low-concentration atropine shows significant therapeutic promise, regulatory complexities present notable challenges to market expansion. </p><p>Additionally, the approval process for myopia-specific formulations varies significantly across international markets. Some regions require extensive local clinical trials even when substantial evidence exists from global studies, delaying product availability. These regulatory nuances create market fragmentation and increase the compliance burden for manufacturers seeking multi-country commercialization.</p><p>
