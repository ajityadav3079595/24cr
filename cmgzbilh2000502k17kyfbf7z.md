</p><p>Deuterium oxide, commonly known as heavy water or deuterium water, is a compound comprising two atoms of deuterium (D) and one atom of oxygen (O), yielding the chemical formula D2O. Deuterium is a stable isotope of hydrogen that contains both a proton and a neutron in its nucleus, whereas the more common hydrogen isotope, protium, has only a proton. This additional neutron makes deuterium twice as heavy as protium, hence the name "heavy water."</p><p>
</p><p>Physically, deuterium oxide resembles regular water in appearance, being a colorless and odorless liquid under standard conditions. However, its density, melting point, and boiling point are slightly elevated compared to H2O. With a specific gravity of approximately 1.10775 at 25°C, a melting point of 3.82°C, and a boiling point of 101.42°C, D2O exhibits increased hydrogen bonding and molecular association due to the mass difference between deuterium and protium.</p><p>
</p><p>Heavy water plays a critical role in nuclear reactors as a neutron moderator, slowing down neutrons so they can sustain a nuclear chain reaction in natural uranium. It is also used in various scientific and medical research applications, including nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy and biological tracer studies. As scientific and technological demands expand, the importance and applications of deuterium oxide continue to grow, driving its global market development.</p><p>
