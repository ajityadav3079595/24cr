 Definition</p><p>
</p><p><strong>Silk Fibroin (SF)</strong> is a protein derived from silkworm silk that has exceptional mechanical, biocompatible, and biodegradable properties. It is widely used in various industries, including biomedical applications, cosmetics, textiles, and food products. SF's unique characteristics, such as high tensile strength and elasticity, make it a preferred material in tissue engineering, drug delivery systems, and medical sutures. Due to its growing demand in regenerative medicine and bioengineering, the market for SF is expected to expand in the coming years.</p><p>

<strong>Restraints:</strong></p><p>

	</p><p><strong>High Production Costs</strong> – The extraction and purification process of SF is expensive, making products costlier compared to synthetic alternatives.</p><p><strong>Limited Raw Material Availability</strong> – The dependency on sericulture for silk fibroin limits production scalability.</p><p><strong>Stringent Regulatory Policies</strong> – The approval process for medical-grade SF applications is complex and time-consuming.</p><p>
<strong>Opportunities:</strong></p><p>

<strong>Challenges:</strong></p><p>

	</p><p><strong>Lack of Standardization</strong> – Variability in silk fibroin sources and processing methods poses challenges in maintaining uniform product quality.</p><p><strong>Competition from Alternative Biomaterials</strong> – Synthetic and plant-based biomaterials compete with SF in biomedical applications.</p><p>
